About this site

Hey, I’m Manish Raghavendra. I made this for fun, but also for that very specific “just in case everything goes sideways” scenario. You know… zombie apocalypse, Fallout-style wasteland, random power grid collapse, or the day you realize supermarkets aren’t as immortal as they look.

This database stores plants and growing profiles, basically survival cheat sheets for growing food in weird situations. Think: “potatoes during a drought,” “tomatoes in containers on a balcony,” or “whatever still grows when life gets… interesting.”

The glossary below explains all the fancy labels and dropdown terms in plain human language, so you don’t need a botany degree (or a functioning civilization) to understand it.

Tiny serious note: this is definitely not a “how to survive radiation” manual. If things ever get real, listen to actual experts and not a guy mixing plant data with apocalypse planning.

Life cycle (annual / perennial / biennial)

  • Annual: Completes its life in one growing season and usually dies with frost or harvest. You replant from seed (or starts) next year. Example: many melons, tomatoes, beans.
  • Perennial: Lives multiple years; the same plant (or its roots/crown) comes back. Example: many fruit trees, asparagus, rhubarb.
  • Biennial: Two-year rhythm: often leaves/root in year one, then flowers and sets seed in year two, then the plant is done. Example: some onions, kale can behave like a biennial if left in the ground.

Environment (the “scenario” dropdown)

This answers: Under what kind of conditions is this advice meant? Examples in the database might include:

  • Home garden: Normal backyard conditions with reasonable care.
  • Drought / water rationing: You must save water; heat stress is more likely.
  • Container-only: You are only growing in pots or grow bags (not in the ground).
  • Urban soil: You might have unknown contamination; testing and raised beds are common themes.
  • Speculative scenarios: marked clearly on the site as “what if” or modeling style rows, not a guarantee of what will work in a disaster or radiation event.

Growing medium

What are you growing in? For example: ordinary garden soil, a raised bed filled with soil/compost, a container, hydroponics (nutrient water, no soil), or similar. The same plant can have different profiles for different media.

Difficulty (beginner / intermediate / advanced)

  • Beginner: Forgiving crop or setup; fewer tools and less precision needed.
  • Intermediate: Needs more attention: timing, watering, pests, or equipment.
  • Advanced: Demanding: tight climate control, sterile technique (e.g. some mushrooms), or high risk of failure.

Confidence (peer_reviewed / field_practice / speculative)

This is how strong the evidence is behind that profile, not how hard the plant is to grow.

  • peer_reviewed: Tied to formal research (journal articles, rigorous studies). In this app you should still list sources so readers can check.
  • field_practice: Standard advice from agricultural extension, experienced growers, or reputable manuals, even if every line was not published as a paper. This is “what professionals and serious gardeners usually do.”
  • speculative: Hypothetical, modeling, or disaster-prep style notes. Treat as ideas to research further, not proven instructions. Profiles marked speculative may still list sources for background reading.

Why the CMS forces sources for non-speculative profiles: If you say the advice is peer_reviewed or field_practice, you must pick at least one source so the entry is not just anonymous text on the internet.

Source types (extension / peer_review / grey_literature / community)

When you add a Source in the CMS, you classify it:

  • extension: University extension, government agriculture pages, and similar public education material (often .gov / .edu).
  • peer_review: Academic journal articles or other work that went through scientific peer review.
  • grey_literature: Credible but not a journal paper: agency reports, technical bulletins, NGO PDFs, conference proceedings.
  • community: Forums, blogs, social posts. Useful for ideas, but weakest as evidence. Use sparingly for serious growing claims.

Summary

Optional short paragraph: what this profile covers in one glance (“Deep containers + mulch for drought potatoes,” etc.).

Climate / region (worldwide)

Each growing profile can include a free-text field for anywhere on Earth: country or state, wet/dry season (e.g. India kharif/rabi), monsoon timing, elevation, a Köppen code (e.g. Aw, Cwa), a RHS hardiness rating if you use UK references, “highland tropics,” and so on. No single global standard fits everyone, so this box is intentionally flexible.

USDA hardiness zone min / max (optional, US-focused)

These two number fields are optional and refer only to the USDA plant hardiness map (mainly the United States, sometimes cited in Canada). They describe typical coldest winter temperature. Lower numbers mean colder winters (e.g. zone 4 is colder than zone 9).

If you garden outside that system (for example much of India, Africa, or Europe using other references), you can leave USDA min/max blank and put everything important in Climate / region plus summary and requirements.

Sources (checkboxes on a profile)

These link the profile to rows in Sources (title + URL, etc.).

  • If confidence is speculative, sources are optional.
  • If confidence is peer_reviewed or field_practice, you must tick at least one source, or the form will not save. That keeps “serious” profiles honest.

Toxicity / safety notes

Free text on the plant (not the profile): parts that are poisonous, green potatoes, oxalates in greens, etc. This is a reminder field, not a complete safety database. When in doubt, check extension and medical poison control resources.
